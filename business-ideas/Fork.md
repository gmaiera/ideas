# Fork

Status: `researching`

## Possible Names

- Fork
- Piggyback

## One-Sentence Pitch

Fork builds consented AI operating twins for established service companies, creating a parallel AI-native version of their workflows that can sell, intake, draft, route, and increasingly execute work while the incumbent partner delivers the regulated or human-critical portions.

## Tagline

Primary draft: "We want to fork you."

Safer external variant: "Your company, forked into an AI-native operating layer."

Piggyback variant: "AI growth and operations, piggybacking on companies people already trust."

## Naming Notes

Fork feels sharper, more technical, and more provocative. It suggests creating a parallel branch of an existing company, which fits the digital twin concept well. The downside is that it may feel threatening to partners if not framed carefully.

Piggyback feels friendlier and more partnership-oriented. It suggests riding alongside an incumbent's brand, trust, licenses, and delivery capacity. The downside is that it may sound less ambitious or less proprietary unless the story makes clear that Piggyback is building the AI-native operating layer behind the partnership.

Working distinction:

- Fork: stronger for the technical thesis.
- Piggyback: stronger for the partner-facing relationship pitch.


## Core Idea

Fork partners with an existing company, maps how the company actually operates, and builds an AI-first mirror of its business. The partner keeps its brand, trust, licenses, and human delivery capacity. Fork brings AI infrastructure, process capture, workflow automation, customer acquisition experiments, and the ability to run a parallel operating model.

The result is a digital operating twin: one version of the company runs through people and legacy systems; the other runs through AI agents, structured workflows, integrations, and human review gates. Over time, more of the repeatable work moves into the AI operating layer.

## Example Wedge

Fork approaches a large accounting firm without hiring accountants first. Instead, Fork proposes a pilot:

- The accounting firm grants explicit access to selected workflows, communication channels, meeting transcripts, SOPs, templates, and historical anonymized work product.
- Fork maps the firm's service delivery process and builds an AI-native intake, drafting, routing, QA, and client communication layer.
- Fork also tests demand generation online for a clearly scoped service line.
- When Fork sells work, the accounting firm performs or supervises the accounting work where licensed human judgment is required.
- Fork earns a share of revenue from new business and/or cost savings created through the AI operating layer.

## Customer

Initial customer: mid-size to large professional services firms with repeatable work, high labor leverage, and meaningful process documentation.

Most promising verticals:

- Accounting and tax advisory
- Legal services with standardized intake and document workflows
- Insurance brokerage
- HR, payroll, and benefits administration
- Compliance and audit support
- B2B customer support or managed services

## Product

Fork can start as a partnership and services company, then become software.

Initial product modules:

- Process mirror: maps workflows from approved sources and interviews
- AI intake: turns leads and client requests into structured work packets
- Drafting layer: produces first drafts, checklists, memos, filings, proposals, and client responses
- Routing layer: decides what needs human review, specialist input, or direct execution
- QA layer: compares AI output against partner standards and regulatory requirements
- Demand engine: tests landing pages, outbound, paid acquisition, and partner-branded offers
- Operating dashboard: shows work volume, cycle time, AI contribution, human review load, revenue, and exceptions

## Business Model

Possible commercial structures:

- Revenue share on new customers sourced by Fork: Fork earns 20-40 percent of gross revenue for a defined period.
- Margin share on automated delivery: Fork earns 30-50 percent of verified margin improvement.
- Platform fee plus success fee: partner pays a monthly platform fee, plus Fork earns a smaller revenue share.
- Joint venture: Fork and the incumbent create a new AI-native service line with pre-agreed ownership economics.

For the accounting example, a reasonable first pilot could be:

- Fork-sourced new revenue: 30 percent to Fork, 70 percent to the accounting firm.
- Partner-sourced existing clients using Fork automation: 15-25 percent of incremental gross margin to Fork.
- Setup fee: optional, waived if the partner gives enough access and commits to a pilot.

## Why Now

AI can now perform a growing share of the coordination, drafting, research, summarization, extraction, and workflow routing that used to require junior knowledge workers. At the same time, many incumbent firms have trust, distribution, licenses, and domain expertise, but lack the internal urgency or technical ability to rebuild themselves around AI.

Fork sits between those two facts: it lets established companies become AI-native without asking them to destroy their current operating model on day one.

## What Makes It Interesting

- It does not need to beat incumbents head-on; it borrows their trust and operating history.
- It starts with real processes rather than abstract AI demos.
- It can create new revenue for the partner, not just reduce cost.
- Each partner engagement can create reusable vertical playbooks.
- The company can evolve from agency-like implementation into infrastructure for AI-native franchising of service businesses.

## Risks And Constraints

- Data access must be explicit, permissioned, logged, and contractually governed. Blind-copying emails or collecting meeting transcripts without informed consent is not a viable operating model.
- Confidentiality, privilege, PII, trade secrets, and regulated work need strict controls.
- The partner may fear Fork is learning enough to compete with them.
- The economics need to reward the incumbent for brand, trust, and delivery capacity while still giving Fork enough upside.
- Some service lines cannot be fully delegated to AI because licensed professional judgment remains required.
- Quality failures could harm both Fork and the partner.

## Better Framing

Instead of saying Fork secretly copies the company, say:

Fork creates a governed AI operating twin from consented operational data, partner-approved workflow observation, structured interviews, and supervised pilots.

Instead of selling "replacement," sell:

- parallel AI operations
- new revenue capacity
- faster delivery
- lower junior-work bottlenecks
- measurable process intelligence
- a way to test the future without breaking the current business

## Open Questions

- Is Fork a venture studio, an AI implementation firm, a SaaS platform, or a joint-venture machine?
- Which vertical has the best combination of repeatable workflows, high margin, low regulatory friction, and hungry partners?
- Does the first partner need to be large, or would a mid-market firm move faster?
- Should Fork co-brand new services or stay invisible behind the incumbent?
- How much of the company can be mirrored from historical data versus interviews and live workflow observation?
- What minimum access is required to create a useful operating twin?

## First Validation Steps

- Pick one vertical, probably accounting or legal-adjacent services.
- Define one narrow workflow, such as bookkeeping cleanup, tax document intake, payroll onboarding, audit request collection, or monthly client reporting.
- Write a one-page partner pitch focused on new revenue and operational leverage.
- Interview 5 partners or operators in that vertical.
- Build a no-code or low-code demo of the AI intake and work-packet creation flow.
- Model three partnership economics scenarios.

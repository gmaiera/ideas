# Fork / Piggyback Partner Pitch - Research Draft

Status: Research draft
Created: 2026-04-26
Purpose: Partner-facing pitch with condensed action plan, economics, and execution roadmap

## Recommendation

Use `Piggyback` for partner-facing conversations and keep `Fork` as the internal thesis name.

Why:

- `Fork` is intellectually stronger: it captures the idea of creating an AI-native branch of an existing service business.
- `Piggyback` is commercially safer: it tells incumbents that the company rides with their trust, brand, license, customer relationships, and delivery capacity.

External phrase:

```text
Piggyback builds consented AI operating layers on top of service companies people already trust.
```

Internal thesis:

```text
We fork the operating model, not the relationship.
```

## One-Page Partner Pitch

### The Problem

Professional service companies have trust, customers, brand, regulatory knowledge, and domain expertise. What they often lack is an AI-native operating layer that can intake work, prepare work packets, draft responses, route exceptions, measure effort, and learn from every delivery cycle.

Most AI pilots stay trapped as demos because they do not connect to the actual workflow: emails, documents, client intake, handoffs, review, approvals, billing, follow-up, and management reporting.

### The Opportunity

Generative AI is now good enough to automate or accelerate large parts of coordination, summarization, extraction, drafting, research, and workflow routing. McKinsey estimated that generative AI could add $2.6 trillion to $4.4 trillion in annual economic value globally, with a large share of impact in customer operations, marketing and sales, software engineering, and R&D. Market reports also put adjacent service pools in very large categories: global legal services around the trillion-dollar range, global business process outsourcing around the hundreds-of-billions range, and accounting/professional services as large recurring markets.

The wedge is not "replace professionals." The wedge is:

```text
create new revenue and higher delivery leverage inside businesses that already own trust.
```

### The Proposal

Piggyback partners with an established service firm and builds a governed AI operating twin for one narrow workflow.

The partner contributes:

- brand and customer trust;
- domain expertise;
- licensed or human-critical judgment;
- consented examples, templates, SOPs, and review standards;
- access to selected operators for workflow mapping.

Piggyback contributes:

- AI intake and work-packet creation;
- workflow mapping;
- AI drafting and routing;
- quality gates and audit logging;
- demand-generation experiments;
- operating dashboard;
- continuous improvement loop.

### First Beachhead

Recommended first vertical: accounting/tax-adjacent operations.

Recommended first workflow:

```text
monthly client reporting and bookkeeping cleanup intake
```

Why this wedge:

- high repetition;
- clear document and data intake;
- obvious handoff and review steps;
- measurable cycle time;
- easy to create synthetic demo data;
- partner can keep licensed judgment and final delivery;
- strong pain around junior-work bottlenecks and client follow-up.

Alternative first workflows:

- tax document intake;
- payroll onboarding;
- audit request collection;
- legal document intake;
- insurance renewal packet preparation;
- compliance evidence collection.

| Market signal | Why it matters for Piggyback |

| Global legal services are commonly reported around the $1T scale. | Legal-adjacent intake, drafting, evidence collection, and routing are huge workflow pools, even if regulated judgment remains human. |
| Global BPO is commonly reported above $300B annually. | Many outsourced processes are structured, repetitive, measurable, and suitable for AI-augmented operating layers. |

The important conclusion:

```text
Piggyback does not need to capture a market. It needs to prove a repeatable economic wedge inside one trusted service partner.
```

## Economic Model

### Pilot Partner Example

Assume a mid-market accounting or compliance partner:

- annual revenue: $10M-$50M;
- target pilot workflow volume: 100-500 client work packets/month;
- current fully-loaded handling cost per work packet: $30-$150;
- average revenue per completed packet or related service cycle: $150-$1,000;
- initial AI contribution target: 20%-40% time reduction on repeatable prep, intake, drafting, routing, and follow-up work;
- human review remains mandatory before client delivery.

### Commercial Structures

Option A: New revenue share

- Piggyback sources or helps convert new customers.
- Partner delivers or supervises regulated/human-critical work.
- Piggyback earns 20%-40% of gross revenue for a defined period.

Option B: Margin improvement share

- Partner uses Piggyback on existing workflows.
- Piggyback earns 20%-40% of verified incremental gross margin.

Option C: Platform plus success fee

- Monthly fee: $2,500-$15,000 for the first operating layer.
- Success fee: 5%-15% of new revenue or verified savings.

Option D: Joint venture service line

- Used only after one workflow proves demand and delivery economics.
- Partner contributes brand, domain, and delivery.
- Piggyback contributes AI operating layer and growth engine.

### Conservative First-Year Scenario

```text
5 partner pilots
3 convert to paid
$5K/month average platform fee
$60K/year per partner recurring
$180K ARR baseline
plus success fees or revenue share
```

This is not venture-scale yet. It proves willingness to pay.

### Better First-Year Scenario

```text
10 partner pilots
6 convert to paid
$10K/month average platform + services
$720K ARR/services run-rate
2 partners launch new AI-native service lines
$100K-$300K additional revenue share potential
```

The inflection point is not the first dashboard. It is the first partner who says:

```text
this workflow now moves faster, costs less, produces cleaner client work, and gives us a new thing to sell.
```

## What Piggyback Actually Builds

### Product Surface

1. AI intake
   - turns emails, forms, PDFs, spreadsheets, and notes into structured work packets.

2. Work-packet generator
   - creates task summary, required inputs, missing items, risk flags, draft output, and review checklist.

3. Human review layer
   - assigns reviewer, captures edits, tracks approval, stores final version.

4. Quality and policy layer
   - checks required fields, consistency, tone, regulatory caveats, and partner-specific standards.

5. Demand engine
   - landing pages, outbound copy, referral copy, and follow-up sequences for the selected service line.

6. Operating dashboard
   - work volume, cycle time, AI contribution, human review load, revenue, exceptions, and rejected outputs.

7. Learning loop
   - every edit becomes signal; every exception updates the playbook; every successful packet becomes training material.

### Data Required

Minimum viable data:

- 10-20 anonymized examples of completed work;
- SOP or process description;
- intake form or email examples;
- final deliverable templates;
- review checklist;
- common missing information;
- common mistakes;
- partner-specific tone and standards;
- approval rules.

Data governance line:

```text
No blind scraping, no hidden monitoring, no unauthorized inbox capture, no customer data without explicit scope and consent.
```

## 90-Day Roadmap

### Days 1-10: Vertical And Workflow Lock

Deliverables:

- choose first vertical;
- choose one workflow;
- define partner profile;
- write consent and data-access checklist;
- create demo dataset schema;
- write partner interview script.

Exit criteria:

- one workflow selected;
- one measurable outcome selected;
- five operator/partner interviews scheduled or identified.

### Days 11-25: Demo Work-Packet Flow

Deliverables:

- synthetic dataset;
- AI intake form;
- work-packet generator;
- human review checklist;
- before/after workflow map;
- dashboard mockup.

Exit criteria:

- can demo one work item from intake to review packet in under 5 minutes;
- partner understands the value without a technical explanation.

### Days 26-45: Partner Pilot Design

Deliverables:

- data governance checklist;
- statement of work;
- pilot success metrics;
- pricing proposal;
- risk register;
- security appendix.

Metrics:

- cycle-time reduction;
- review edit rate;
- missing-info reduction;
- cost per packet;
- partner satisfaction;
- new-revenue conversion, if demand engine is included.

### Days 46-75: Live Pilot

Deliverables:

- 50-100 real or anonymized work packets;
- weekly operator review;
- dashboard;
- exceptions log;
- approved/rejected AI output tracking;
- revenue or savings estimate.

Exit criteria:

- partner can say which part of the work improved;
- partner can identify which pieces still require human judgment;
- Piggyback can price the workflow with evidence.

### Days 76-90: Productization Decision

Deliverables:

- case study draft;
- repeatable workflow playbook;
- reusable agent/workflow template;
- pricing update;
- next partner target list;
- decision: continue, pivot vertical, or narrow workflow.

## First Partner Pitch Script

```text
We are not asking you to replace your team or hand over your business.

We want to take one workflow that already repeats inside your firm and build a governed AI operating layer around it.

You keep the client relationship, brand, licensed judgment, and final approval.

We help intake the work, create structured work packets, prepare drafts, route exceptions, track review, and measure exactly where time and quality improve.

The first pilot is narrow: one workflow, consented examples, synthetic or anonymized data where needed, human review on every output, and a dashboard that shows cycle time, review load, AI contribution, and exceptions.

If it works, you get a faster service line and potentially a new AI-native offer to sell.

If it does not work, we leave you with a clearer workflow map, better intake checklist, and evidence about where AI is not yet ready.
```

## Objections And Responses

### "Are you trying to learn enough to compete with us?"

No. The model depends on the partner's trust, brand, customer relationship, and licensed/human judgment. Piggyback makes money by expanding partner capacity, not by stripping the partner out.

Contractual response:

- non-compete or non-circumvention for defined customers/workflows;
- data-use restrictions;
- no reuse of confidential partner material outside the pilot;
- anonymized generalized learnings only with approval.

### "Our data is sensitive."

Correct. That is why the pilot starts with scoped, consented, anonymized or synthetic material where possible. Real customer data enters only after the partner approves the data boundary, retention period, access list, and deletion/export procedure.

### "AI quality is not good enough."

The first goal is not autonomous delivery. The first goal is better intake, faster packet prep, clearer missing-item detection, and reviewable drafts. Human approval remains the gate.

### "This sounds like consulting."

The first engagement includes consulting because the workflow must be understood. But the asset produced is reusable software, workflow templates, QA checklists, and a dashboard. The goal is to turn repeated work into a productized operating layer.

### "Who owns the output?"

Default partner-friendly answer:

- partner owns customer deliverables;
- Piggyback owns the platform and generalized workflow engine;
- partner confidential data remains partner property;
- reusable templates derived from non-confidential learnings can be negotiated.

## Data Governance Checklist

- What customer data is in scope?
- Who authorized access?
- Is data anonymized, synthetic, or real?
- Where is data stored?
- Who can access it?
- How long is it retained?
- Can the partner export it?
- Can the partner delete it?
- Are prompts and outputs logged?
- Are human edits logged?
- Which outputs require human approval?
- Which outputs are prohibited?
- Are customer-facing uses disclosed?
- What happens after pilot termination?

## Condensed Action Plan

1. Pick accounting/tax-adjacent operations as the first test unless a warmer partner appears elsewhere.
2. Build one synthetic demo around monthly reporting or bookkeeping cleanup intake.
3. Interview five operators or partners.
4. Convert one interested firm into a narrow 30-day pilot.
5. Price the pilot as platform plus setup or zero setup plus upside, depending on partner access.
6. Measure cycle time, missing-info rate, review edits, and willingness to pay.
7. Decide after 90 days whether Piggyback is a venture studio, AI implementation firm, or productized workflow platform.

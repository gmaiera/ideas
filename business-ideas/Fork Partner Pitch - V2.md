## Executive Pitch

Piggyback builds consented AI operating layers for established service companies.

The partner keeps the customer relationship, brand, professional judgment, and regulated delivery. Piggyback maps one repeatable workflow and adds an AI-native layer for intake, work-packet creation, drafting, routing, QA, review, follow-up, and measurement.

The first product is not a generic chatbot and not a consulting report. It is a workflow operating twin.

```text
We do not replace the firm. We make one valuable workflow inside the firm faster, more measurable, and easier to sell.
```

## Why This Matters

Professional services firms are full of high-value work trapped behind manual coordination:

- documents arrive incomplete;
- junior staff chase missing information;
- experts repeat the same explanations;
- drafts wait for review;
- partners have weak visibility into bottlenecks;
- clients experience delay without knowing why;
- firms cannot easily turn expertise into a scalable operating asset.

AI can help, but only if it is embedded into the workflow with the right data, policy, review, and accountability.

That is Piggyback's wedge.

## Market Frame

This is a large-opportunity thesis with a deliberately narrow starting point.

The relevant markets include:

- professional services;
- accounting, tax, payroll, and compliance;
- legal and legal-adjacent document workflows;
- BPO and managed services;
- insurance brokerage and renewal workflows;
- regulated customer operations.

The macro signal is clear: generative AI has large economic potential in knowledge work, customer operations, sales, software-like workflows, and business support functions. The investable question is narrower:

```text
Can Piggyback prove that one trusted service partner will pay for measurable workflow leverage?
```

## Beachhead Decision

Recommended first vertical:

```text
accounting / tax-adjacent services
```

Recommended first workflow:

```text
monthly client reporting and bookkeeping cleanup intake
```

Why:

- recurring monthly cadence;
- repeatable documents;
- clear missing-information loops;
- measurable cycle time;
- high review burden;
- easy synthetic demo data;
- human professional judgment remains protected;
- partner can turn faster workflow into a service promise.

What not to start with:

- broad firm-wide transformation;
- full autonomous delivery;
- regulated final advice;
- multiple verticals;
- vague AI strategy workshop.

## Unit Economics Hypothesis

Assume one workflow with 200 packets/month.

Before Piggyback:

- 45 minutes average prep and chase time per packet;
- 150 human hours/month;
- fully loaded coordination cost of $35-$75/hour;
- monthly coordination cost: $5,250-$11,250.

After Piggyback target:

- 25%-40% reduction in prep/chase/rewrite time;
- 37.5-60 hours/month saved;
- $1,300-$4,500/month visible cost leverage, before revenue upside;
- additional value from faster turnaround, better client experience, and clearer management visibility.

This is not enough by itself to justify a venture-scale platform. It is enough to justify a paid pilot if the workflow is painful and repeated.

The upside appears when:

- the same workflow repeats across many clients;
- the partner sells an improved service promise;
- the operating twin becomes reusable across partners in the same vertical;
- Piggyback owns the workflow software and playbook, not just the labor.

## Product V2

### Core System

1. Intake parser
   - reads email, forms, PDFs, spreadsheets, and notes;
   - extracts entities, missing items, deadlines, and risk flags.

2. Work-packet generator
   - creates summary, checklist, draft output, missing-info request, and reviewer notes.

3. Review cockpit
   - shows draft, source evidence, confidence flags, and required human signoff.

4. Partner policy layer
   - encodes style, standards, disclaimers, approval thresholds, and prohibited claims.

5. Operating dashboard
   - shows volume, cycle time, missing info, AI contribution, review edits, exceptions, and outcomes.

6. Learning loop
   - captures edits, rejections, and repeated exceptions as improvements to the workflow.

7. Demand layer
   - optional landing page, outreach, and follow-up assets for the improved service line.

### First Demo Dataset

Create 12 synthetic clients:

- 4 clean monthly reporting cases;
- 4 missing-document cases;
- 2 discrepancy cases;
- 1 urgent executive request;
- 1 edge case that should be escalated to a human.

Each case should include:

- client profile;
- incoming email;
- document list;
- transaction summary;
- missing information;
- desired output;
- review checklist;
- final packet.

## Trust Model

Piggyback survives only if partners believe it is not trying to steal the business.

### Partner Protections

- scoped workflow;
- defined data boundary;
- explicit data-use terms;
- no reuse of confidential material;
- human approval on final output;
- partner owns customer deliverables;
- audit log of AI outputs and human edits;
- export/delete path after pilot;
- non-circumvention for defined accounts/workflows where appropriate.

### Data Boundaries

Allowed in pilot:

- synthetic data;
- anonymized historical examples;
- partner-approved templates;
- SOPs;
- review checklists;
- selected sample communications.

Not allowed without explicit approval:

- raw inbox capture;
- customer PII;
- privileged legal data;
- regulated financial data;
- live client communications;
- hidden recording or transcript collection.

```text
We want to run one narrow experiment with you.

Pick a workflow that repeats, creates coordination drag, and still needs your team's judgment.

We will map it, build an AI operating twin around it, and show you whether the work can move faster without losing control.

Piggyback handles intake structure, work-packet creation, drafts, missing-info detection, review support, dashboarding, and the learning loop.
```

## Investor Lens

### What Could Become Venture-Scale

Piggyback becomes interesting if it can turn one-off partner workflow pilots into:

- repeatable vertical playbooks;
- shared workflow software;
- reusable QA/policy modules;
- standardized data schemas;
- partner distribution;
- revenue share from AI-native service lines.

### What Could Stay Services-Heavy

Risks:

- every partner workflow is too custom;
- data access negotiations take too long;
- gross margins depend on human setup;
- partner trust requires high-touch delivery;
- verticals differ too much.

### Proof Needed

Before calling this a software company, prove:

- one workflow repeats across at least three partners;
- setup time decreases from pilot to pilot;
- AI review quality improves from captured edits;
- partner pays recurring fees;
- value is measurable without heroic manual analysis.

## 90-Day Roadmap V2

### Sprint 1: Thesis To Demo

Duration: 10 days

Deliverables:

- one selected workflow;
- synthetic dataset;
- workflow map;
- demo work-packet generator;
- dashboard mockup;
- partner pitch one-pager.

Acceptance:

- a partner can understand the pilot in under 5 minutes.

### Sprint 2: Partner Discovery

Duration: 15 days

Deliverables:

- interview 5 operators/partners;
- record pain, volume, current cost, review rules, and buying objections;
- choose one pilot candidate;
- update pricing hypothesis.

Acceptance:

- one partner says "this is worth piloting" or the wedge changes.

### Sprint 3: Pilot Design

Duration: 15 days

Deliverables:

- data access agreement outline;
- pilot scope;
- success metrics;
- review workflow;
- security checklist;
- pricing proposal.

Acceptance:

- partner approves pilot scope and data boundary.

### Sprint 4: Pilot Execution

Duration: 30 days

Deliverables:

- 50-100 packets processed;
- review edits captured;
- dashboard updated weekly;
- exceptions analyzed;
- value report produced.

Acceptance:

- measurable improvement or clear reason to stop.

### Sprint 5: Scale Decision

Duration: 20 days

Deliverables:

- case study draft;
- reusable workflow template;
- next 10 partners list;
- software roadmap;
- decision: continue, pivot, or stop.

Acceptance:

- one of three outcomes:
  - expand same partner;
  - repeat with another partner;
  - kill or reframe the wedge.

## Go / No-Go Criteria

Go if:

- one workflow has clear repeated pain;
- a partner will provide consented examples;
- value can be measured in hours, revenue, quality, or cycle time;
- human review keeps risk controlled;
- setup work can become reusable.

No-go if:

- partner only wants generic AI advice;
- no data access is possible;
- workflow is rare or bespoke;
- risk requires full human rewrite every time;
- economics only work with heavy consulting labor.
